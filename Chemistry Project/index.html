Rocket Propellants

Powering Spaceflight Through Chemistry

Written and Presented by Riley Santor

Explore the Chemistry ↓
🔥

Solid Propellant

Fuel and oxidizer are pre-mixed into a solid grain. Once ignited, it burns until fully consumed. Used in boosters like the Space Shuttle SRBs.

💧

Liquid Propellant

Fuel and oxidizer stored separately as liquids, then mixed in a combustion chamber. Allows throttle control. Used in engines like the RS-25.

⚡

Hybrid Propellant

Combines a solid fuel grain with a liquid or gaseous oxidizer. Simpler than liquid systems but more controllable than solid.

Section 01

5 Vocabulary Words

Related to rocket propellant chemistry — defined in my own words

Propellant
A material that is burned or decomposed to produce hot gases that push a rocket forward. It includes both the fuel and the oxidizer working together.
Oxidizer
A chemical that provides oxygen for the fuel to burn. In space, there is no air, so rockets carry their own oxidizer (like liquid oxygen or ammonium perchlorate).
Combustion
A fast chemical reaction between a fuel and an oxidizer that releases heat and light energy. It's what happens when the propellant ignites inside the rocket engine.
Thrust
The force that moves a rocket forward, created by burning propellant and pushing hot exhaust gases out through the nozzle at high speed (Newton's Third Law).
Specific Impulse
A measure of how efficiently a rocket engine uses its propellant. Higher specific impulse means the engine gets more thrust per unit of propellant burned.
Section 02

Chemicals Involved

The chemicals used in a liquid hydrogen/oxygen rocket engine (like the Space Shuttle Main Engine RS-25):

H2 Hydrogen (liquid fuel) — provides the energy when it combines with oxygen
O2 Oxygen (liquid oxidizer) — supports combustion by reacting with hydrogen
H2O Water (gaseous product) — high-temperature steam expelled as exhaust to create thrust
How They Work Together:
Liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen are stored in separate tanks at extremely cold temperatures (−253°C and −183°C). They are pumped into the combustion chamber at high pressure where they mix and ignite. The resulting exothermic reaction produces superheated water vapor (steam) that expands rapidly and exits through the nozzle, generating thrust.

Section 06

Type of Chemical Reaction

🔥 Combustion Reaction

General Pattern: Fuel + O₂ → Oxide products + Energy

This is a combustion reaction because hydrogen (the fuel) reacts with oxygen to produce water and releases a large amount of heat and light energy. Combustion reactions are always exothermic — they release energy.

In a rocket engine, this energy is what produces the extremely hot gases that expand and create thrust. The hydrogen-oxygen combustion is one of the most energetic chemical reactions used in rocketry, with flame temperatures reaching over 3,000°C.

Section 09

Explanation of the Process

In my own words

Rocket propulsion works by using a chemical reaction to create thrust. In a liquid rocket engine like the RS-25 (used on the Space Shuttle and NASA's SLS rocket), two chemicals are used: liquid hydrogen (the fuel) and liquid oxygen (the oxidizer).

These chemicals are stored in separate tanks at extremely cold temperatures — hydrogen at −253°C and oxygen at −183°C — to keep them in liquid form. Powerful turbopumps force the two liquids into the combustion chamber at very high pressure.

Inside the combustion chamber, the hydrogen and oxygen mix and are ignited. The chemical reaction 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O takes place incredibly fast. This is a combustion reaction that is highly exothermic, meaning it releases an enormous amount of heat — the flame temperature can exceed 3,000°C.

The heat turns the water product into superheated steam that expands rapidly. This expanding gas is forced out through a specially shaped nozzle at the bottom of the engine. According to Newton's Third Law (for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction), pushing the hot gas downward creates an upward force — this is thrust.

The beauty of hydrogen-oxygen rockets is that the only exhaust product is water vapor, making it one of the cleanest rocket fuels available. The RS-25 engine produces about 2.28 million Newtons of thrust in vacuum and has one of the highest specific impulses (~452 s) of any chemical rocket engine.

Section 10

Why This Chemistry Matters

✅ Advantages

  • Highest specific impulse of any chemical propellant — very efficient
  • Only byproduct is water vapor — the cleanest rocket fuel
  • Hydrogen and oxygen are abundant and can be produced from water
  • Throttleable — engines can be controlled precisely
  • Proven technology used in the Space Shuttle, SLS, and Ariane rockets

❌ Disadvantages

  • Liquid hydrogen is extremely cold and hard to store (−253°C)
  • Hydrogen has very low density — requires huge fuel tanks
  • Complex engine design with high-speed turbopumps
  • Expensive to produce and handle safely
  • Cannot be stored for long periods — boils off over time
Impact on the World:
Rocket propellant chemistry has made space exploration possible. From the Apollo missions that took humans to the Moon to the International Space Station and modern commercial spaceflight, these chemical reactions are the foundation of our ability to leave Earth. Understanding combustion chemistry is also critical for developing cleaner, more sustainable propulsion technologies for the future — including hydrogen fuel cells for cars and green energy production. The same H₂ + O₂ reaction that launches rockets could one day help replace fossil fuels on Earth.
