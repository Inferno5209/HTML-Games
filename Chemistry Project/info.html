📋 Presentation Script

Introduction: "Hi, I'm Riley Santor, and my chemistry project is about rocket propellants — specifically the liquid hydrogen and oxygen propulsion system used in engines like NASA's RS-25."


Vocabulary: "Before we dive in, here are five key vocabulary words. A propellant is the material burned to produce thrust. An oxidizer provides the oxygen needed for combustion in space. Combustion is the rapid chemical reaction between fuel and oxidizer that releases energy. Thrust is the forward force created by exhausting hot gases. And specific impulse measures how efficiently the engine uses its fuel."


Chemical Reaction: "The main reaction is 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O. In words: hydrogen gas plus oxygen gas yields water. This is a combustion reaction — it's exothermic, meaning it releases a huge amount of energy as heat and light."


Process: "Liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen are stored in separate tanks at extremely cold temperatures. Turbopumps force them into the combustion chamber where they ignite. The resulting superheated steam exits through the nozzle at high speed, creating thrust through Newton's Third Law."


Conclusion: "Rocket propellant chemistry is essential to space exploration. The hydrogen-oxygen system is one of the cleanest and most efficient available, and the same reaction principles are being applied to hydrogen fuel cells for sustainable energy on Earth."

✅ Advantages of Hydrogen-Oxygen Propulsion

  • Highest Performance: Liquid hydrogen/oxygen engines have the highest specific impulse (~450 seconds) of any chemical rocket, meaning maximum thrust per unit of fuel.
  • Clean Exhaust: The only combustion product is water vapor (H₂O), making it the most environmentally clean rocket fuel available.
  • Abundant Resources: Hydrogen and oxygen can be produced through electrolysis of water, making them potentially renewable fuel sources.
  • Throttleable: Liquid engines can be throttled up, down, or shut off completely, allowing for precise control during flight.
  • Reusable: Modern liquid engines (like SpaceX's Raptor concept derivatives) can be restarted and reused multiple times.
  • Proven Reliability: Used successfully for decades in the Space Shuttle, Saturn V upper stages, Ariane 5, and now the SLS rocket.

❌ Disadvantages of Hydrogen-Oxygen Propulsion

  • Extreme Cold Storage: Liquid hydrogen must be stored at −253°C, requiring expensive insulated tanks and constant cooling.
  • Low Density: Hydrogen is the lightest element — even as a liquid, it has very low density, requiring enormous fuel tanks that add weight and size.
  • Boil-off Problem: Liquid hydrogen slowly evaporates even in insulated tanks, making long-term storage impractical for deep space missions.
  • Complex Engineering: The engine requires high-speed turbopumps, precision injectors, and careful thermal management — making it expensive and complex to build.
  • Hydrogen Handling: Hydrogen is highly flammable and can cause hydrogen embrittlement in metals, requiring special materials and safety protocols.
  • High Cost: Producing, storing, and handling liquid hydrogen is significantly more expensive than alternatives like kerosene-based fuels.

🔍 Additional Research Notes

Future of Rocket Propulsion: While hydrogen-oxygen remains the most efficient chemical propellant, researchers are exploring alternative propulsion methods including:

  • Methane-Oxygen Engines: SpaceX's Raptor engine uses methane (CH₄) instead of hydrogen — easier to store and potentially manufacturable on Mars.
  • Ion Propulsion: Uses electric fields to accelerate charged particles — extremely efficient but very low thrust, suited for deep space missions.
  • Nuclear Thermal Propulsion: Uses a nuclear reactor to heat propellant — could cut Mars travel time in half.
  • Green Propellants: NASA is testing non-toxic propellants to replace hazardous hydrazine in spacecraft thrusters.
