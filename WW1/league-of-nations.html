THE DAILY CHRONICLE

March 19, 1919 | Washington, D.C. | Price: 2 Cents

NATION DIVIDED: Will America Join Wilson's League of Nations?

Senate Battle Looms as Debate Over International Commitment Intensifies

PRO A New Era of Peace: Why America Must Join the League

By James Mitchell, Washington Correspondent

President Wilson's vision of a League of Nations represents humanity's best hope for preventing another catastrophic war. After witnessing the unprecedented devastation of the Great War, which claimed millions of lives and destroyed entire nations, America has a moral obligation to lead the world toward lasting peace.

The League offers a forum where nations can resolve disputes through dialogue rather than warfare. Through collective security, member nations would stand together against aggression, making war far less attractive to potential aggressors. America's participation would give the League the strength and credibility it needs to succeed.

Those who advocate for isolation ignore the reality that modern warfare respects no borders. The next conflict will inevitably draw America in, just as this one did. By joining the League now, we can help prevent that conflict from ever starting. Our soldiers fought "to make the world safe for democracy"—abandoning the League would betray their sacrifice.

AGAINST Preserving Our Sovereignty: The Dangers of Foreign Entanglement

By Senator Henry Cabot Lodge, Guest Columnist

The proposed League of Nations threatens the very foundations of American independence. Article X of the League Covenant would obligate the United States to defend any member nation against aggression, effectively surrendering Congress's constitutional power to declare war to an international body dominated by foreign powers.

Our Founding Fathers wisely warned against "entangling alliances" that could drag America into foreign conflicts that do not serve our national interests. For over a century, this policy has kept us free and prosperous. The League would reverse this successful strategy, binding us to intervene in every European squabble and colonial dispute across the globe.

America has just emerged from a devastating war that cost us dearly in blood and treasure. Our priority must be rebuilding our own nation, not policing the world. The League is an idealistic dream that ignores human nature and the realities of international power politics. America must remain master of its own destiny.

Editorial Stance: AGAINST the League
This cartoon depicts Uncle Sam walking away from the League of Nations building, choosing the path of isolationism instead. The crossroads represents America's critical decision between international engagement and maintaining traditional isolation from foreign entanglements. By turning his back on the League, Uncle Sam symbolizes the nation's ultimate rejection of Wilson's vision and its return to the protective policy of staying out of European affairs.
